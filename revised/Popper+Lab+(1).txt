\begin{abstract}
This experiment examined the relationship between the work done on a popper toy and the maximum height it reached during its launch, using the principle of conservation of energy. Several different amounts of force were applied to the compression of the popper, and the vertical displacement was measured. We used these measurements to calculate the work done on the popper. Video analysis was utilized to find the maximum height for each launch. Results revealed that increasing the amount of work applied to the popper would lead to an increase in its maximum height, and although some of the energy was not conserved in the popper specifically, the results support the law of conservation of energy.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The introduction should work from broad to specific. Give major important background information or set up the major concepts to be used or tested. Set up the key research questions or hypotheses to tested. Optionally, give top level overview of method to be used. 

Energy, work, and force are critical in understanding physics. Energy is the capacity to do work and change, and work is the energy transferred to an object when force causes it to move. Elastomeric toys like poppers serve as excellent models for demonstrating conversion of energy on a macroscopic scale. A popper is a hemispherical elastomeric toy that stores elastic potential energy when mechanically inverted and compressed.Upon release, a restorative force returns the toy to its original geometry, converting the stored potential energy into kinetic energy to launch it vertically.  This study investigates the mechanics of these devices.

According to the work-energy theorem, the net work done on a system (in our case the popper) equals its change in kinetic energy. Therefore, increasing the work done on the elastic system should proportionally increase its maximum kinetic energy and likewise the max height of the object. However, this does not apply to all objects or is different (Ex, a rocket in space moving despite it running out of fuel due to Newton’s first law, or a feather falling slower than a rock despite any more effort you put into slamming it into the ground).
The purpose of this experiment is to see if more work is put into the popper (i.e., “applying a greater initial downward force” or “increasing the initial compression”) then the popper's maximum height will increase, and it will launch further. By measuring its displacement vs the force applied to the popper and calculating the potential and kinetic energy of the popper, we can determine if the popper does launch further with more force and therefore work applied to it. Therefore, we hypothesized that increasing the work applied to compress the system would result in a proportional increase in maximum launch height.
The purpose is also to see if energy is conserved in the popper when launching it, and explore the Law of Conservation and Energy, where energy cannot be created or destroyed. Since all objects seemingly follow this law, we will see if the popper also follows this.

\section{Methods and materials}
This experiment utilized a 5.7-gram elastomeric popper (see below), and to measure its height, two meter sticks were taped to the wall on top of each other to cover the full distance the popper could cover. To record the data, an Apple iPhone was used at 60 frames per second, 3 feet away from the metersticks (to get a full view of the popper when it launches), and manually launched the popper at relatively similar popper compressions. This was then repeated 10 times. To determine the maximum height of the popper, we would go frame by frame in the video to determine its peak, using the meterstick as a reference. To get precise measurements for the force applied to the popper, a scissor jack (See Fig. 1) was used and the popper was placed under it. The scissor jack would slowly lower while the popper is on top of a scale to measure its weight. The weight would be recorded to estimate the amount of force being exerted on it.

\section{Discussion}
The results support the hypothesis we initially proposed that changing the amount of work done on the popper changes the maximum height. This phenomenon occurs because greater work stores more elastic potential energy in the popper. For the conservation of energy portion of our introduction, the mechanical energy of the popper is not conserved, however the total energy is conserved. A t-test confirmed a statistically significant difference between the work applied (.1451 J) and the kinetic energy at launch (.0682 J). This indicates that the mechanical energy of the system is not conserved. This 53% energy loss is dissipated with non-conservative forces, such as internal thermal friction, sound energy, and drag during launch phase.
Several minor inconsistencies were also found in the data, which can be due to air resistance, inconsistent alignment of the popper, or video recording limitations. This can also be an explanation  as to why the mechanical energy conserved was “lost” due to energy being converted as heat or sound, conveying that not all work done was converted into upward motion.

In conclusion, the experiment demonstrated the conservation of energy and the direct relationship between work and maximum height in a system consisting of a popper and the Earth.
